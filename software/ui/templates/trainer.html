Step 3 · Parameters
Epochs 60
How many full passes the model makes through your data.
💡 More epochs = the model practices more. Better results, but takes longer.
What it is
One epoch = one full pass of the optimizer through every training example. Each pass updates the model's weights via gradient descent — nudging each value in the direction that reduces error. More epochs → better fit, but risk of overfitting: memorising your data instead of learning general patterns.
In your model
In comparison
A homework drill: 5–50 passes. Research fine-tuning: 100–500 passes. GPT-4 was trained on 1.8 trillion tokens — seen roughly once, but each token effectively acts as a unique "example" in a single enormous epoch.
Learning Rate
How big the weight updates are after each batch.
What it is
The learning rate (α) is the step size in gradient descent. After each batch: w -= α × gradient. Too high → loss oscillates and diverges. Too low → training stalls and never converges.
In your model
In comparison
Fast (0.01): aggressive, good for quick experiments. Steady (0.001): the go-to default for most neural nets. Careful (0.0001): fine-tuning or fragile models. GPT-4 used a peak lr of ~6×10⁻⁴ with a cosine decay schedule — tuned over months of ablation runs.
Latent Dimension 256
Size of the model's internal feature space. Bigger = richer patterns, slower training.
What it is
In a Variational Autoencoder (VAE), the latent space is a compressed numerical vector your model uses to "think about" an image. The encoder squeezes your image into this vector; the decoder expands it back. A larger latent dimension gives more room to store visual detail.
In your model
In comparison
Tiny research VAEs: 32–64 dims. Standard image VAEs: 128–512 dims. Stable Diffusion's latent space: 4×64×64 = 16,384 dims. GPT-4's internal state: ~12,288 dims per token position — but those encode language, not pixels.
Window Size 40
How many tokens the model sees at once when learning patterns.
What it is
The context window (sequence length) is how many tokens the LSTM sees in one forward pass. Longer windows let the model learn longer-range patterns, but use more memory and slow each training step. Tokens here are subword units from GPT-2's tokenizer — roughly 0.75 words per token on average.
In your model
In comparison
Short-text LSTM: 20–40 tokens ≈ a sentence. GPT-2: 1,024 tokens ≈ a full article. GPT-4: ~128,000 tokens ≈ a short novel. The longer the window, the more the model can "remember" when generating — but training cost scales with every extra token.
Batch Size
How many examples processed before each weight update.
What it is
Batch size is how many examples are processed before one gradient descent step. Smaller batches → noisier but more frequent updates, which can help regularisation. Larger batches → smoother gradients, but need more memory.
In your model
In comparison
Debug runs: batch 1–2. Fine-tuning LLMs: 2–8 (memory-limited). Full pretraining runs of GPT-4: estimated millions of tokens per batch across thousands of GPUs — each gradient step aggregates an enormous amount of signal.
Data Augmentation 50×
Synthetic copies of each example, with random transforms.
What it is
Data augmentation synthetically expands your training set by applying random transforms — flips, rotations (±10°), brightness/contrast jitter — to each image. This forces the model to learn invariant features rather than memorising exact pixel values, acting as a powerful regulariser against overfitting.
In your model
In comparison
No augmentation (1×): fine for huge datasets. Moderate (5–10×): standard for small image classifiers. Heavy (50–100×): used in medical imaging with tiny annotated sets. Large-scale models like GPT-4 don't need augmentation — their training corpus is already billions of diverse examples.
Batch Size
How many examples processed before each weight update.
What it is
Batch size is how many images are processed before one gradient descent step. Small batches (4–8) produce noisier but more frequent updates — this noise acts as implicit regularisation and helps escape shallow minima. Large batches (16–32) give smoother gradients and faster GPU throughput, but can overfit quickly on tiny datasets.
In your model
In comparison
Debugging: batch 1–4. Common practice: 32–128. Large-scale vision models (ResNet, ViT): 256–4,096. GPT-4 pretraining: millions of tokens per step across thousands of GPUs. The live image grid shows exactly your batch size of generated samples each epoch.

Lesson 1 · Image Generator · VAE

Dream Machine

Train a model on photos from your world — your school, your plants, your bedroom — then watch it dream up new images it has never seen before.

What you'll learn

  • What generative models do — and how they differ from classifiers
  • What a latent space is: the model's internal "imagination map"
  • What reconstruction loss is — how the model measures its own output quality
  • Why generated images get blurry with small datasets — and what that tells you about scale
  • What mode collapse is, and why it happens with tiny training sets
  • Why brainy has no "pre-trained" option for image generation — and what training Stable Diffusion actually cost

The project

You'll upload 10–30 photos of something personally meaningful — your school courtyard, your houseplants, your face, your cat. Then you'll train a Variational Autoencoder (VAE): a generative model that learns to compress each image into a small vector of numbers (the latent space), then reconstruct it back. Once trained, it can generate new images by sampling from that space — images it has never actually seen.

Here's the constructionist insight wired into this project: the results will probably be blurry. That's not a failure — it's data. Stable Diffusion trained on 2.3 billion images for 90 days on 4,000 GPUs. You trained on 20 photos for 3 minutes on a laptop. The blurriness is the model showing you, visually, how much it doesn't know. That's reconstruction loss made visible.

[lesson preview: live training canvas showing blurry-to-less-blurry progression across epochs]

Step-by-step walkthrough

1
Gather your photos

Take 20–30 photos of your subject. Vary the angle, lighting, and distance. All images are resized to 96×96 internally — tight, centred crops work better than wide shots with complex backgrounds.

2
Open Image Generator mode

On the brainy home screen, select Image Generator. Upload your photos. Brainy accepts JPG, PNG, or webcam captures. The data step is complete when you can see thumbnails of all your images.

3
Tune parameters

Start with Epochs = 60, Latent Dimension = 128, Augmentation = 50×, Batch Size = 8. Augmentation is especially important here — it synthetically triples your dataset by flipping, rotating, and adjusting each image, which helps prevent the model from memorising rather than learning.

4
Train and observe

Watch the live preview. Epoch 1: blobs. Epoch 20: rough shapes. Epoch 60: recognisable features, still soft. If every generated image looks the same (mode collapse), you need more variety in your training photos or a lower latent dimension.

5
Generate and reflect

In the Test tab, generate 20+ samples. Compare variety vs sharpness across different parameter runs. The "Your Model vs Big AI" panel shows exactly how your training compares to Stable Diffusion on compute, data, and time.

6
Deploy

Save and deploy to the Pi. A button press generates a new dream. Connect a small screen or thermal image printer — the Pi becomes an art installation that endlessly hallucinates variations of your world.

Lesson 2 · Text Generator · LSTM (from scratch)

Misfortune Teller

Train a tiny language model on your own collection of small dreads — then let it generate new ones. This is your first sequence model: the same core idea that powers predictive text on your phone.

What you'll learn

  • What a sequence model is — predicting the next token, one step at a time
  • How text becomes numbers: vocabulary, tokens, and embeddings
  • What epochs, learning rate, and window size each do for an LSTM
  • Why small datasets produce repetitive output — and the two ways to fix it
  • How to wire a trained model to a thermal printer or speaker on a Pi

The project

A misfortune teller is a text generator trained on a list of small, specific dreads — the kind that feel darkly funny when written down. "You will laugh before you realise the story was serious." "You will find the perfect word fifteen minutes after the conversation ends." Write 50–100 of them, each one sentence, train the model, and it will generate new ones in your exact style.

This project works because the training data is yours: personal, specific, and short. LSTM models trained on small datasets learn to recombine and mutate what they've seen. That tends to produce output that feels slightly unhinged in a good way — the model becomes a strange echo of your own internal monologue.

Final step: hook it to a thermal printer or speaker on your Pi. Press a button, get a misfortune. Give it to a friend. Watch their face.

[lesson preview: brainy text training UI with loss curve and sample output appearing over time]

Step-by-step walkthrough

1
Write your misfortunes

Aim for 50–100 sentences, each one line. Start with "You will…" or a variation. Be specific — "you will lose your charger" is worse than "you will find your charger behind the radiator, three days after you bought a replacement." Specificity is what gives the model something to learn.

2
Choose Text → Scratch (LSTM)

On the brainy home screen, select Text Generator. On the next screen, choose From scratch. This trains a compact LSTM directly on your examples — no pre-existing model knowledge, no borrowed weights. Just your sentences.

3
Tune parameters

Paste in your sentences one by one. Set Epochs = 80–100 (small datasets need more passes), Learning Rate = Steady, Window Size = 30–40 tokens. Hit Train.

4
Watch the loss curve

Loss should drop steeply in the first 20 epochs, then gradually level off. Sample output at each checkpoint starts as gibberish, becomes partially grammatical, then develops your voice. If it never becomes coherent after 80 epochs: add more examples or shorten the window size.

5
Test with seed phrases

In the Test tab, enter "You will" as a seed and generate. Try temperature low (0.4) for predictable output, high (1.0+) for chaotic mutations. The sweet spot is usually 0.7–0.9 — coherent but surprising.

6
Deploy

Save your model. Deploy to Pi. Wire a button to trigger generation, a thermal printer or text-to-speech to output it. The Pi becomes a pocket oracle that dispenses your anxieties back at you, mutated.

Lesson 3 · Text Generator · Smart Prompt (pre-trained)

Screenplay Writer

Mix lines from movies, plays, and books you love — and let the bot write new stories in that mashup style. Same category as Lesson 2 (text generation), completely different method.

What you'll learn

  • What RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is — and why it's not the same as training from scratch
  • Why pre-trained models exist, and when to use them vs train from zero
  • What embeddings are — how text gets turned into meaning vectors so similar ideas cluster together
  • Why this method uses no epochs, no gradient descent, and no loss curve
  • How prompt engineering and example curation shape what the model outputs — two levers that replace hyperparameter tuning

The project

You'll curate 30–50 lines or short passages from stories you love — Shrek and Hamlet and a Studio Ghibli film, if that's your mix, or anything else. Then you tell the bot to write new scenes using that mashup as its voice. Ask it: "write a scene where the dragon meets the prince." Watch what it does.

Under the hood, brainy converts each of your examples into an embedding: a list of ~384 numbers that mathematically encodes its meaning. When you send a prompt, brainy finds the 3–5 examples that are semantically closest to it and injects them into the context sent to the language model (LLaMA 3.2 via Ollama). The model generates a response that's grounded in your examples, not just its training data.

This is Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Nothing is trained. No weights change. The magic is entirely in your curation — which examples you choose, how specifically you describe the style, how many you provide. The bot is only as interesting as the library you give it.

[lesson preview: brainy chat interface with style examples visible in the sidebar]

Step-by-step walkthrough

1
Choose your blend

Pick 2–4 source styles you want to mix. They should be different enough to be interesting together: a 16th century play and a 2001 DreamWorks film, for instance. The contrast is what makes the output surprising.

2
Curate your examples

Write or copy 30–50 short passages in those voices — lines of dialogue, short monologues, scene descriptions. Each is one brainy entry. Variety of topic matters: love, fear, food, battle, small talk. The retrieval works better when it has coverage.

3
Choose Text → Smart Prompt

Select Text Generator → Smart Prompt on the brainy home screen. Paste your examples. Write a system prompt that describes the style blend: "You write in the combined voice of Shakespeare and Shrek. Formal sentence structure, but crude vocabulary. Every speech is secretly about honour."

4
Prepare (no training)

Hit Prepare My Bot. Brainy embeds your examples — this takes 10–30 seconds. There is no loss curve, no epochs, no gradient descent. The moment the embeddings are ready, your bot exists. This is the key difference from Lessons 1 and 2.

5
Write scenes

In the Test tab, send prompts like "write a scene where two characters argue about bread" or "write an opening monologue for a villain who is also very polite." Watch whether the bot adopts your blend. If it sounds generic: sharpen the system prompt. If it ignores your examples: the examples may not be similar enough to your test queries — add more varied ones.

6
Deploy

Save and deploy to the Pi. Wire a keyboard or microphone for input and a screen or speaker for output. The Pi becomes a collaborative writing partner with the combined voice of every story you gave it.
